In the lab and in the classroom, field lines are a useful way to visualise a vector field. To construct a field line, we simply draw a line in space which is tangent to the field vector at each point along its path. As well as being used to make bad album art, field lines can, in some situations, take on physical significance. One example is in plasma physics, where the Lorentz force acts on charged particles to effectively limit their motion to along magnetic field lines. This effect is put to good use in magnetic confinement fusion (MCF) experiments (e.g. the tokamak, where the magnetic field geometry is carefully designed so as to minimise contact between the hot plasma and the cool reactor walls. This is the reason that all modern MCF experiments are shaped like doughnuts.

The operators of a tokamak produce the required magnetic field (B-field for short) using external coils and also, indirectly, by driving electrical current within the plasma itself. Since it plays such a big role in fusion performance, great care is taken to measure the B-field accross the plasma volume during an experiment, and this data is studied in detail when the experiment breaks is not running. We are interested in whether the measured B-field is as expected, since a large deviation means that something is wrong, either with the experiment or with our understanding of the physics involved.
